Our Story

A Letter from
Our Pastor

Pastor Namju Bae

For over forty years, this congregation has gathered on the northwest side of Chicago — a community of Korean-American families spanning grandparents, parents, and children, carrying their faith across generations and across an ocean.

But a church with history still has to ask: what are we becoming?

✦

A Mother's Faith

My conviction about the church began not in a seminary classroom but in childhood.

My mother was not a Christian before she married. Then my older brother became seriously ill — a condition no one could explain or cure. God healed him. That was the beginning of her faith.

What followed was something I watched as a young boy: my mother attending church faithfully, week after week, traveling a long distance, through difficulty, without giving up. She never made a performance of it. She simply kept showing up.

I did not fully understand then what I was seeing. I understand now. She was being the primary faith teacher in our home — not by lecturing, but by living. Her faith was visible. It shaped mine.

That image has never left me.

The Family We Are — and the Families We Serve

I grew up in Korea, completed my university education there, and came to the United States as an adult. Korean is still the language I think in. My wife grew up here — English is her language. Our child is growing up in both.

There are many families like ours in this city.

In these families, Sunday morning presents a quiet but real problem. Grandparents are most comfortable in Korean. The younger generation reaches more naturally for English. Parents are somewhere in between, code-switching through the week. And so when it comes to church, the family splits — different services, different congregations, different communities of faith. Each person goes where the language feels right.

I understand why this happens. But I believe it quietly breaks something.

If the whole family cannot worship together, they cannot grow together. They cannot carry the same text home. They cannot have the conversations that build faith across a dinner table. The family that worships apart tends to believe apart — and in the hard seasons, that distance shows.

This is why I built Worship Translation — a live AI interpretation tool that allows a sermon preached in one language to be received simultaneously in another. It is not a perfect solution. But it is my attempt to remove one of the real barriers that keeps Korean-American families from worshipping in the same room, hearing the same Word, together.

A family that can worship together can stay together in faith. That is what I am trying to build.

What I Am Building Here

I am dreaming of a family-centered church — and I mean that in a specific way.

When grandparents, parents, and children worship together — when the Sunday school class, the youth group, and the adult congregation are all meditating on the same passage on the same Sunday — something happens that no church program can manufacture. The family drives home with a shared text. It comes up at dinner. A grandmother mentions something the passage reminded her of. A teenager asks a question they wouldn't have asked a pastor. A parent realizes they need to actually know what they believe in order to answer it.

The pastor's role is not to replace that conversation. It is to resource it.

On Teaching

I have spent many years studying Scripture — an M.Div. from a Methodist seminary, two Th.M. degrees from Baptist and Lutheran institutions, and doctoral work in Old Testament at a Baptist school. I grew up in the Presbyterian church and was ordained in the Christian Reformed Church.

I pursued that education because I believe the church suffers most when it mishandles the Word of God.

I chose Old Testament studies because without a correct understanding of the Old Testament, the entire Bible remains partially closed. The New Testament is the fulfillment of what the Old Testament promised. You cannot fully understand the cross without understanding the sacrificial system. You cannot fully understand grace without understanding the law. You cannot fully understand the Kingdom without understanding the covenant.

I also remember the moment hermeneutics finally made sense to me. I had taken the course several times. Then once, I looked at the table of contents of the assigned textbook and saw that the final chapter was on application. It reoriented everything. All the tools of interpretation exist for one purpose: to help people live. God did not give us the Bible merely to be studied. He gave it to be lived.

That is what I am trying to preach — the whole Bible, correctly understood, honestly applied to the lives people are actually living. I work as a web developer for the state of Illinois during the week. I know what Monday looks like. The sermon has to be able to survive Monday.

What We Believe About the Church's Purpose

There is a phrase in theology: already but not yet.

The Kingdom of God is not only a future hope. It broke into history when Jesus came. It is present now — in communities of faith, in acts of mercy, in the proclamation of the gospel, in families who love one another well. We do not merely endure this life while waiting for heaven. We participate in what God is already doing on earth.

And yet the Kingdom is not yet complete. Christ has not yet returned. Suffering is real. Justice is incomplete. We hold our work and our hopes with open hands, knowing that God alone builds what lasts.

This means our congregation is called to both: to enjoy the real blessings of life before God on this earth, and to spend ourselves in missionary work, in serving neighbors, in proclaiming the gospel — because this world matters to God, and so do the people in it.

We are not a church that tells people to simply wait for heaven. We are a church that tries to live the Kingdom now, together, as families, one Sunday at a time.

An Honest Invitation

If you are looking for a church where things are kept simple and comfortable — where the sermon is short and the expectations are low — this may not be the right fit.

But if you are a Korean-American family navigating two languages, two cultures, and two generations under one roof — and you are looking for a place where all of you can belong, grow, and worship together — then we would love to meet you.

We are a small congregation. We know each other's names. We have been through hard seasons together and we expect more ahead. But we are here, and we are building something we believe is worth building.

Come and see.
