Our Church

Our Story
and Vision

Pastor Namju Bae

Our name, Light and Love, grows out of a question that has shaped our church from the beginning.

What kind of church are we dreaming of becoming?

We dream of a church where families grow together in faith, where every generation shares the same Word of God, and where Scripture is faithfully interpreted and lived out in real life.

We are asking what calling God has entrusted to Light and Love Church in this time and in this place.

✦

A Dream That Began with My Mother's Faith

My dream for the church did not begin in a seminary classroom. It began in the memories of my childhood.

Before marriage, my mother was not a Christian. Then my older brother became seriously ill. No one could clearly explain the cause, and no one could easily treat his condition. At that time, surprisingly, a doctor encouraged my parents to go to church. By God's grace, my brother recovered. That became the beginning of my mother's faith.

What I saw as a young boy after that was my mother's steady faith. She went to church every week without fail. She traveled a long distance, endured difficult circumstances, and did not give up. She did not try to draw attention to herself. She was simply, faithfully there.

At the time, I did not fully understand what I was seeing. Now I do. My mother was living as the spiritual teacher of our home. Not merely by the words she taught, but by the faith she lived. Her faith was visible. And that faith shaped my own.

I also remember seeing families who attended church together, and I saw how precious and beautiful that was for a family. That picture has never left my heart.

Faith is a personal decision. But faith is also passed down, shared, tested, and strengthened within the family. That is why I dream of a church where families grow together in faith.

A Church Where the Whole Family Grows Together in Faith

Today, it is becoming increasingly difficult for an entire family to attend the same church. Work, distance, language, culture, children's educational environments, and generational differences often cause families to attend different churches, or even within the same church, to become scattered across different worship services and communities.

We understand why this happens. Each generation has different needs, languages, and environments. But at the same time, we must take this question seriously:

If a family cannot share the place of faith together, how can faith be passed on to the next generation?

Everyone can go through seasons of spiritual dryness. At times, people lose interest in church. At times, their concern for faith grows weak. But if grandparents, parents, children, and even extended family can share faith within one church, they can help hold one another up during those difficult seasons.

Pastors give direction, teach the Word, and serve the congregation. But the closest examples of faith for children are their parents. When parents first stand before the Word, pray, worship, and show faith through their lives, children learn what faith means.

Faith does not remain only inside the church building. It grows around the family table, in daily conversations, and through seasons of difficulty that families walk through together.

So we dream not merely of a church where families attend together, but of a church where families grow together in the Word.

A Church Where Every Generation Shares the Same Word

The church we dream of is not necessarily a church where every generation always worships in the same room in the same way. There may be special Sundays when all generations worship together. But the deeper direction is this:

Every generation meditating on the same Scripture passage during the same week.

Children, youth, young adults, and adults may each have worship gatherings and groups that fit their stage of life. But the Scripture passage they share can be the same. Pastors and teachers can teach the same biblical text in ways that are appropriate for each generation.

When this happens, conversations about faith can naturally continue at home.

Parents can ask their children:
“What did you learn from today's passage?”

Children can ask their parents:
“What did this Word mean to you?”

Grandparents can share the grace of God they have experienced throughout their lives. Younger generations can bring their questions about school, work, relationships, identity, and the future before the Word of God.

When a family shares the same Word, faith becomes more than something that happens on Sunday. It becomes part of the life of the home. The church does not replace that conversation. The church helps make that conversation possible.

A Bridge for Korean Immigrant Families

Our church stands within the reality of Korean immigrant families.

In some families, there are grandparents who have lived their faith in Korean. In some families, there are 1.5-generation adults who grew up between Korea and America. In others, there are second-generation children who were born and raised in the United States and are more comfortable in English. And younger children are growing up between Korean and English, between two cultures.

This is not merely a language issue. It is a family issue. It is a faith issue.

Many families ask practical questions.

“Should we worship in Korean?”

“Should we worship in English?”

“Should parents and children attend different services?”

“How can one family live out faith together?”

I do not know this struggle only in theory. I grew up in Korea, finished college there, and came to the United States as an adult, so Korean is still more natural to me. My wife grew up in America, so English is more natural to her. Our child was born and is growing up here, between two languages and two cultures.

This is the reality of our family. It is also the reality of many immigrant families.

That is why we want to help families avoid being divided in faith simply because of language and culture. Each person may have a language that feels more natural, but that does not mean a family's faith must be divided.

To support this vision, we are also making practical efforts through technology. I created a program that uses AI to provide real-time sermon translation while the message is being preached. This is not merely a technical experiment. It is a pastoral effort to help families hear the Word together, worship together, and remain spiritually united.

We do not want language differences to divide a family's faith. We want the church to become a bridge across those differences.

A Church That Teaches Scripture Faithfully

Another important part of the church we dream of is faithful teaching of Scripture.

I have had the opportunity to study the Word of God in several seminaries and theological traditions. I studied in Methodist, Baptist, and Lutheran contexts through M.Div., Th.M., and Ph.D. studies in Old Testament. I also grew up in a Presbyterian church and was ordained in the Christian Reformed Church, the CRC.

These diverse denominational and theological experiences helped me see the breadth and depth of the Christian faith. At the same time, they planted one conviction more deeply in me:

The church must handle the Word of God carefully, honestly, and faithfully.

I especially value the Old Testament. Without a faithful understanding of the Old Testament, it is difficult to understand the whole Bible rightly. The New Testament opens upon the promises and longings of the Old Testament. Without understanding the sacrificial system, it is difficult to understand the cross fully. Without understanding the law, it is difficult to understand grace fully. Without understanding covenant, it is difficult to understand the kingdom of God fully.

Today, many sermons do not listen deeply to the meaning of the biblical text. Instead, a few verses are sometimes used to support the preacher's own thoughts. But the church does not need merely good words or inspiring ideas. The church needs the Word of God.

Wrong interpretation can lead to wrong teaching. And wrong teaching can prevent people from seeing the true gospel.

That is why we dream of a church that honors the biblical text, clearly proclaims the gospel, and faithfully teaches the will of God.

A Church That Understands the Word and Lives It Out

Faithful understanding of Scripture does not stop with knowledge. The Word must lead to life.

I studied hermeneutics several times. But its meaning came home to me most deeply during my final hermeneutics class. One day, I looked at the table of contents of the textbook. After many chapters explaining how to interpret Scripture — how to examine context, history, genre, and theology — the final chapter was titled “Application.”

In that moment, one thing became clear.

The goal of biblical interpretation does not end with the question, “What does this text mean?” It moves toward the question, “How should we live through this Word?”

All the tools of interpretation — language, context, historical background, literary genre, and theological flow — move toward one purpose: helping God's people live according to his Word.

God did not give us the Bible merely to study it. He gave it to us so that we might live it. He gave us his Word so that we might know him, trust him, worship him, and live rightly before him.

The pastor's calling does not stop at interpreting Scripture. A pastor must also help the congregation consider how to live God's Word in the real world — in family, work, suffering, relationships, parenting, culture, technology, and society.

During the week, I work as a web developer for the State of Illinois, and I face real-world problems there. So I know what Monday looks like. I know the weight of work, the responsibility of family, the complexity of life, and the weariness of reality.

A sermon should not only sound gracious on Sunday. It must be able to endure Monday. More than that, it must help people live Monday by faith.

We dream of a church where faithful interpretation of Scripture and real-life application go together.

A Church Living in the Hope of the Kingdom of God

There is a theological phrase:

Already,
but not yet.

The kingdom of God has already begun through the coming of Jesus Christ. Jesus died on the cross, rose from the dead, and reigns even now. Therefore, the kingdom of God is not only a distant future hope. It is here even now.

It is present in the community of faith.

It is present in acts of mercy.

It is present in the proclamation of the gospel.

It is present in families that love one another well.

It is present in lives that serve their neighbors.

We are not people who merely endure life on earth while waiting for heaven. We are people who participate in what God is already doing in this world.

And yet, the kingdom of God has not yet been completed. Christ has not yet returned. Suffering is still real. Sin still shakes the world. Justice has not yet been fulfilled. We still wait.

That is why Christians hold two directions together. We hope for the kingdom of God that will one day be completed. At the same time, we live today as citizens of that kingdom here on earth.

For that reason, our church wants to receive and enjoy God's grace and blessings on this earth with gratitude. We also want to give ourselves to mission, neighborly service, and the proclamation of the gospel, because this world is precious to God, and the people in it are precious to him.

Our hope is not an escape from reality. Our hope is the strength to live faithfully today.

An Honest Invitation

We are not a perfect church. We are not a large or impressive church. But we are building something we truly believe is worth building.

We do not want to become merely a comfortable religious space. We do not want to promise a light and easy version of faith. Instead, we want to become a community that learns together, wrestles together, grows together, and lives together in the Word.

If you are a Korean American family living between two languages, two cultures, and multiple generations — and if you are looking for a place where your whole family can belong, grow, and worship together — we would love to meet you.

We are a small community. We know one another's names. We have walked through difficult seasons together, and we know there will be more seasons to walk through. But we are here.

A church that connects generations through the Word, builds families through the gospel, and lives out the hope of the kingdom of God in everyday life.

That is the kind of church we want to build together.

Come and see.
